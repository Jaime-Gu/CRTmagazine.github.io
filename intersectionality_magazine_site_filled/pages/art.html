Intersectionality in Manet’s Famous Olympia

Olympia (1963) by Édouard Manet emerges as a particularly compelling site for examining how works of art can articulate inequalities within a coherent visual system. In this painting, issues of gender, race, and class coexist in a relatively explicit manner, yet they are often approached in isolation from one another. However, intersectionality, as theorised by Crenshaw, provides a critical framework through which the interpretive scope of the painting can be expanded, enabling a more nuanced understanding of the mechanisms of inequality that give rise to such a representation.

Hierarchy and relation

The composition constructs hierarchy relationally, making Olympia’s centrality dependent on the maid’s racialised and labouring marginalisation.

Two femininities

The painting stages two differentiated forms of femininity, whose meanings emerge only in relation to one another.

Visibility and gaze

Attention is unevenly distributed: not all bodies are equally visible, and looking itself becomes part of the power structure.

First of all, let’s observe the composition of the painting: it constructs hierarchy relationally. Olympia, reclining, nude, and confrontational, occupies the centre only through contrast with the Black maid, who stands clothed and recedes into the background. Spatial and luminous oppositions (centre/periphery, light/shadow) simultaneously encode gender (exposed body vs working body), race (illuminated whiteness vs shadowed Blackness), and class (sexual transaction vs domestic service). Important thing to understand: these axes do not operate in isolation. Olympia’s centrality is produced through the maid’s racialised and labouring marginalisation. The painting thus reveals inequality as a system of interdependent relations rather than discrete categories.

Second, the painting stages not one but two differentiated forms of femininity, whose meanings emerge only in relation to one another. Olympia’s body is sexualised, frontal, and economically legible, while the Black maid’s body is desexualised, functional, and visually subordinated. This contrast does not merely oppose two women; it structures femininity itself along racial lines, making gender inseparable from race. Olympia’s desirability and relative agency are produced through the presence of a racialised figure assigned to service and partial invisibility. In turn, the maid’s marginalisation stabilises the conditions under which Olympia can appear as both subject and commodity. What the painting reveals, then, is not a universal category of “woman,” but a hierarchised femininity, co-constructed through intersecting relations of race, gender, and power.

Another thing we can look at talking intersectionality, is the way the painting articulates two interconnected economies of the body, structured through both class and race. Olympia participates in a sexual economy in which her body is directly exchanged, looked at, desired, and rewarded (it is supposed that the flower brought by the maid are a present from a man). In opposition, the maid is confined to domestic labour, facilitating that exchange from the margins. The bouquet she carries, passes through her hands, makes her the material intermediary of Olympia’s desirability.

Also, the painting establishes a visual economy of attention: not all bodies are equally visible to the viewer. Rather than simply opposing light and shadow, it regulates how quickly and fully each figure can be apprehended. You, viewer, naturally looked at Olympia first, then the flowers and third the maid (and maybe if you looked for a while you then notice the black cat, hidden in the darkness). Olympia’s body is immediately legible, demanding and sustaining the gaze, while the maid’s presence is less accessible, requiring a more attentive or prolonged act of looking. This uneven visibility is not incidental but structural. Whiteness appears as the natural site of attention, while Blackness is rendered peripheral, supporting but not competing with the central figure. What emerges is a visual economy in which to be seen, and how one is seen, is already shaped by intersecting relations of power. Visibility itself becomes a function of inequality.

We can also notice that Olympia confronts the viewer directly, asserting a form of controlled visibility, while the maid remains disengaged from this exchange, positioned outside the primary circuit of the gaze. This asymmetry implicates the viewer. To look at Olympia is to participate in a visual transaction excluding the maid who is looking at Olympia. The gaze is therefore not neutral: it is distributed across unequal positions, revealing how seeing and power are constructed within the image.

Finally, this painting is to be situated in an imperial and colonial context. These intersecting hierarchies exists within a broader imperial and colonial influence, which underpins the scene without being explicitly foregrounded. The presence of the Black maid is not an isolated detail but part of a wider circulation of bodies, labour, and representations shaped by nineteenth-century French colonialism. What appears as an intimate, domestic interior is thus structured by global relations of power. Racial difference, economic function, and gendered roles are not only co-present but historically produced through imperial systems. The painting reveals how inequalities are condensed into a single visual field, where they organise and sustain one another.
