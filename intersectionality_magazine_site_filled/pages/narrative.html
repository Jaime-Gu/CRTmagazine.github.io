Section 03

Tayo and Ryry

A short narrative presented with a quieter, story-led rhythm while keeping the full text from the magazine.

A History of Two ZebrasNarrative mode

In another world, there are two groups of zebras: one with white skin beneath black stripes and the other with black skin beneath white stripes. To the naked eye, they are looked exactly the same without careful inspection. Tayo and Ryry were two young foals, one from each group, and neither knew of the hidden biological differences beneath their fur...

A History of Two Zebras

By the river, they were two friends playing in the mud. They spent their mornings chasing butterflies through the tall reeds, splashing in the cool water, and drying off under the delighted sun. When they looked down at the river surface, they only saw two black-and-white zebras side by side. They ran together every day, with children’s innocence, racing to see who could reach the old willow tree first. Nevertheless, all those cheerful things began to change when they entered school.

That’s their first morning at the Valley Primery School when Tayo and Ryry waited in queue at the front office to get their class schedules. A busy clerk at the desk asked for their names. When Ryry spoke, the clerk nodded, opened a dark-green registry book on the desk, and ticked off his name. Then Tayo gave his name. The clerk paused, looking through the names one by one, but finally after taping to his desk, he frowned slightly, and reached into a bottom drawer. He pulled out a different, thinner registry book with a grey cover. He put a check mark on Tayo’s name there.

“Why are we in different books?” Tayo asked, stretching his neck to see if there were any other books in the drawer.

The clerk didn't look up. “It’s just administrative policy,” he said casually, shuffling some papers. “Different backgrounds are filed in different records. It’s just how the school has always organized things. Next in line, please.”

“It seems like just we would be in different groups Tayo.” Ryry shrugged with a bit pity, but eager to get to class. At that moment, strange knot tightened in Tayo’s chest with a confusing feeling, hard to tell what was that, something strange to him.

Inside the classroom, things kept feeling slightly different in ways Tayo couldn't fully explain as well. During history class, the teacher handed out a heavy book called The Great Valley Journey. She pointed to an illustration of a large, proud herd running across a bright, open field.

“This is how our ancestors found this land and made it safe,” she said. She asked Ryry to read the first page aloud. Ryry read a history about a courageous leader who led zebras cross the raging river. When he finished, the teacher smiled. “Well done, Ryry. It’s a wonderful history, right? We should take the excellent qualities of our ancestors.”

Tayo looked closely at the map on the next page. Tayo looked at the picture. It didn't match the bedtime stories his grandmother used to tell him. In those stories, zebras didn't run through river, but navigated the harsh, windy mountains in the dark to reach sunny field. He raised his hand. “Excuse me,” he said. “My grandma told me that some zebras came over the dark mountains. Is their story in this book too?” The teacher frowned slightly. “Yes, Tayo. There were some through another way, but we foucus on this group of zebras today. It will be on the test. Let's stick to the main part.”

Later that morning, while walking down the hallway to get a drink of water, a hall monitor stepped in front of Tayo. “Where are you supposed to be?” the monitor demanded, eyeing him suspiciously. Tayo had seen Ryry walk down this exact hallway a few minutes earlier without anyone saying a word.

That afternoon, Tayo walked home feeling quiet and heavy. He didn't race Ryry to the willow tree. He went straight into his house and sat by his grandmother, who was mending a blanket by the window. “Grandma,” Tayo said, staring at his hooves. “Am I different from Ryry? Why the office put my name in a different book? And the teacher said the history book is about our ancestors, but it’s totally different what you told me.”

His grandmother stopped her work. She set the blanket down and looked at Tayo for a long time.

“You are all young zebras, Tayo,” she said gently. “But the valley has its own habits. They sort you by the old records they kept in drawers, and the rules they made long before you were born. You are the same and just recorded by different ways.” She picked up her needle, “There isn’t my story in the schoolbook ’cause it’s not their history, it’s just ours. They only crossed river so they never record climbing mountains.”
