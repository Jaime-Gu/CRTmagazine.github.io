Revisiting

Color-Blind Racism

In 2003, Eduardo Bonilla-Silva published a foundational work in the field of critical race theory entitled “Racism Without Racists: Color-Blind Racism and the Persistence of Racial Inequality in the United States”. In the book, Bonilla-Silva analyzed a myriad of interviews—many with college students—to pursue an answer to a simple question: what does racism look like in the post-civil rights United States?

Though Bonilla-Silva’s book was a pivotal text in both critical race theory and critical whiteness studies, 2003 was two decades ago. Bonilla-Silva had no way of knowing that the country he was writing about would undergo a new metamorphosis; that the sociopolitical sphere and how it interacts with race, racism, and intersectionality would fundamentally change once again. Gay marriage would be legalized, America would elect its first Black president (and then reelect him), the ‘Me Too’ movement would rise to prominence, as would cancel culture. The BLM movement would take on a new prominence following George Floyd’s murder, the pendulum would swing to the ideological right, and a man whose campaign centered on anti-immigrant and anti-DEI policies would win the presidency.

The playing field has been resowed; the goalposts have been moved. Society has changed, but perhaps the most glaring fact is that, in some ways, it hasn’t. Looking back at “Racism Without Racists”, it doesn’t seem dated in any way. Instead, it seems prescient.

The conversations that Bonilla-Silva was starting in 2003—though they have metamorphosed with the times—are more relevant than ever. What follows are interviews with modern college students, aiming to discuss and potentially reframe the same conversations from Bonilla-Silva’s work in the frame of a 2020’s sociopolitical climate. The results are immensely interesting, but I’ll let the words speak for themselves.

Society has changed, but perhaps the most glaring fact is that, in some ways, it hasn’t.

I’m a liberal, but…

Performative liberalism on college campuses is hardly new. College has long been a breeding ground for individual social rebellion disguised as ostensible political leanings. What has grown in modern times, however, is an expression of liberalism as a base coat for further political ideologies. Many of the respondents felt the need to assure the interviewer that they were, in fact, liberal, before discussing contradictory opinions.

Q: What does racism look like on your college campus
Student: “You know...I’m a liberal and everything, but I...I really don’t think we have much of it at our school. Of course you’ll have one or two...um...weird people, but for the most part it’s not really, uh, a thing anymore.”

Announcements of liberalism, seemingly in an attempt to assure the interviewer that they are indeed speaking to someone ‘woke’, precede many arguably illiberal ideas. Here we have the pronouncement of racism as being ‘over.’ Student 1 writes it off as a fringe idea, reserved for “one or two” people. Here we see a common thread: racism is over. Downplaying seems to be the word that fits best here.

Racism, in Student 1’s mind, is neither systemic nor the result of hatred or bias. Rather, it’s attributed to “weird people,” discussed as a personality quirk rather than an implicit bias.

Q: So does that mean racism isn’t an issue anymore?
Student: Well...again, I’m a liberal and I vote Democrat and all...but I just really don’t think it’s, um, cool. People don’t like racism, it’s not the norm anymore.

Veiled—once again, by assurances of liberalism and ‘socially acceptable’ voting patterns, lies a bizarre and seemingly counterintuitive discussion of racism as being ‘uncool.’ Racism is viewed by these students in the same lens as such abstract ideas as ‘popularity’ and ‘cool-ness’. Racism, for many college students, went out of fashion with bullies who shove students into lockers and demand their lunch money. It’s seen as a social behavior, and an adherence to social ‘norms,’ instead of implicit and systemic biases. Thus, after racism’s ‘moment’ had passed—slipping from the overt to the covert—these white students no longer felt the need to acknowledge its existence entirely. But at least we know they’re ‘liberals’.

I’m all for equality…

Though much has changed since Bonilla-Silva’s book, there has been one issue that has remained (if not grown) in the sociopolitical zeitgeist: affirmative action. Affirmative action—or, as the modern political climate has repackaged it, DEI (diversity, equity, and inclusion)—remains a hotbutton issue on which many white students spout opinions with words like “fairness”, “merits”, and “reverse racism.”

Q: Do you think universities should implement an affirmative action policy in their admission process?
Student: I think people should be accepted based on their qualifications...or—um, their merits. On the other side of things, I don’t think I should be penalized just because I’m white.
Q: Do you feel that you’ve been penalized because of your race?
Student: Well, no...I was speaking in the hypothetical. No students should be rejected because they’re White.

At the end of the day, these are smart students who understand the true nature and ideological necessity for some form of remedial solution to education inequalities. This is a fact that is swept under the carpet, so to speak, in these conversations. The discussion isn’t focused on affirmative action uplifting students, but rather on this idea of “penalizing” students who are White.

Bonilla-Silva discusses the rhetorical strategies present in color-blind racism. One here that I would describe is intentional misunderstanding. There is a deliberate recalibration that these students undergo—where they reframe and refocus the conversations around a false equivalence. In this context, equating affirmative action to a penalty for Whiteness.

These interviews are not intended to reveal some hidden truth about closet racists on college campuses. Racism exists, even after slavery, the Civil Rights Movement, and Barack Obama’s election as president. Racism hasn’t disappeared; it has evolved.

These interviews serve to illuminate this idea. Political conversations have grown more nuanced and commonplace in everyday conversation, and so too has color-blind racism. Now, however, it may be better described as racism-blind racism.

Note

The interviews in this article are fictitious, and are intended for narrative and educational effect only.
