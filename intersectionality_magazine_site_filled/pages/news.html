News 1

Transformation of Black Queer Spaces

The Black Queer community have been working towards creating spaces that can connect and celebrate their diverse identity.

Across universities, Black Queer students struggle to find spaces that represent and feel inclusive of their intersectional identity. A recent interview by student and award winning Black Queer writer Taj discusses his difficulty navigating both predominantly cis-het Black spaces alongside White Queer spaces. He notes the challenges with exclusion from both, facing either a lack of cultural understanding and microaggressions from White Queer spaces or Queerphobia from cis-het Black spaces. He highlights the importance networking and building a Black Queer community to help transform these spaces and make them more inclusive for Black Queer students.

One recent example of these spaces is from the University of Manchester’s Queerly Black Society founded in July 2025. They focus on creating a “safe space that connects and supports those who identity with the Black Queer experience.” They also highlight their goal of allowing their members to be their authentic selves without discrimination or ridicule. The recent formation of this society shows the importance of these spaces for Black Queer students to have a space where they feel included and able to fully express themselves.

One space that represents this is the UK Black Pride festival which reaches their 20th anniversary this year. This celebration is considered the world’s largest for Black members of the LGBTQI+ movement. It aims to be an inclusive event that promotes unity with the broader Queer community, showcasing their efforts to transform white dominated LGBTQI+ spaces. One recent interview by student Abi McIntosh highlights the importance of these intersectional spaces and celebrations as she recalls attending UK Black Pride for the first time where she had “never seen so many Black queer people in the same space; it felt as if every queer person of colour in England was there.” This shows how the importance of the recent development of inclusive Black Queer celebrations, offering Black Queers the opportunity to establish communities and find unity in a space that covers all aspects of their intersectional identity.

News 2

Struggles of Black Transgender Women

Black transgender women face some of the most severe forms of marginalisation and abuse, placing them at the centre of discourse surrounding intersectionality.

Recent reports show that Black trans women remain the most targeted group within the transgender community. Notably, a report from the Human Rights Campaign Foundation (HRCF) in November 2025 reveals a shocking disproportionate number of fatal violence against Black Transgender women since 2013. Out of almost 400 reported cases, 59.4% were Black transgender women. This reveals pattern reflects the danger faced by those with intersectional identities like Black transgender women who face hostility rooted in both their sexuality and racial identity.

A column by Shuan Harper discusses the struggle of Black transgender women finding an inclusive and safe space at universities and colleges, highlighting how in interviews, she found that women centres and study programmes felt cantered around white women whilst LGBTQI+ centres focused too little on the experience of Black Queers and the intersectionality of homophobia, transphobia and racism. Furthermore, with recent political developments in nations like the US with anti-DEI policies and the increasing “hateful universal declarations” about transgender Americans, there has been an increased difficulty with establishing and maintain spaces that represent and unite the Black transgender community.

Activist Hope Giselle-Godsey talks about how events like the Transgender Day of Remembrance (TDOR) need to go beyond mourning and become a catalyst for real action and systematic change. She highlights how violence against Black transgender women rooted in social, cultural and institutional failures and therefore remembrance should shift towards prevention and longterm change. She encourages a ‘Blueprint for Change’ including improving safety through holding the police accountable and developing the reporting system for anti-trans violence alongside funding Black trans-led and focused organisations. This shows how action needs to prioritise protecting Black transgender women as the most endangered group in societies, criticising recent protests for disregarding their struggles.

Recent movement ‘Protect the Dolls’ reflects this, phrase popularised during London Fashion Week on the 23rd of February 2025 on white t-shirt designed by American Conner Ives. The term “doll” became a popular way to refer to the trans women community and saw large success with famous celebrities such as Madonna and Pedro Pascal seen wearing the t-shirt. As of September 2025, the official t-shirts raised over $600,000 for charity Trans Lifeline. Despite its success it doesn’t offer direct support to Black transgender women despite the phrase originating from Black dominated Ballroom culture. This shows a need for minority-focused movements to prioritise Black transgender women vulnerable due to their intersectioned identity.
